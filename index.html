Executive Summary

Bandera Ventures invests in early-stage companies building real technology for the physical economy — energy systems, industrial automation, critical minerals processing, and agricultural technology — primarily in Australia and New Zealand.

Australia is one of the most resource-rich, strategically important, and politically stable countries in the world, yet it systematically underinvests in innovation. The result is a market with world-class technical talent, globally relevant IP, and seed-stage valuations 50–70% lower than comparable US deals. Bandera exists to capture this mispricing.

Three converging forces accelerate this opportunity: the global electrification of transportation, industry, and heating; the deployment of AI into physical systems; and the geopolitical imperative to build sovereign supply chains for critical minerals. Australia sits at the intersection of all three.

Why Australia

A Critical Minerals Superpower

Most US investors think of Australia as a commodity exporter — iron ore, natural gas, maybe wine. That's the old story. The new story is that Australia has become one of the most strategically important technology markets in the Indo-Pacific, with direct relevance to US national security, energy security, and supply chain resilience.

#1 Lithium
World's largest reserves — the building block of batteries and energy storage.
#2 Cobalt
Second-largest reserves — essential for semiconductors and defense systems.
AUKUS Aligned
The US–Australia Critical Minerals Agreement and AUKUS alliance make Australia the most obvious allied-nation supply chain alternative to China.

Plus significant deposits of nickel, copper, and rare earth elements. As the US works to reduce dependence on Chinese-controlled supply chains, Australia is the answer.

Why Australia

World-Class Innovation,
Systematically Underfunded

Australia has produced foundational technologies: Wi-Fi, cochlear implants, and high-efficiency photovoltaic solar cells. Its university system is globally ranked. CSIRO — the national science agency — is comparable to DARPA in scope.

But Australia's track record of commercializing these innovations is poor. Capital flows overwhelmingly into real estate and mining, not technology.

Australia's A$3.5 trillion superannuation system — the world's fourth-largest pension pool — overwhelmingly favors listed equities and real estate. Venture capital is a rounding error. The result: extraordinary technical talent and almost no early-stage funding for hardware and deep-tech.

Why Now

Three Converging Forces

01
Electrification of Everything
For the first time in human history, the cheapest way to create energy is harnessing the sun. As the cost gap widens, electrification becomes the dominant trend in transportation, heavy industry, HVAC, and agriculture — driven by economics, not environmentalism. The IRA, CHIPS Act, and DOE funding are creating enormous demand for exactly the technologies Australian founders are building.
02
AI Moves Beyond Software
The next wave deploys intelligence into physical systems: autonomous mining, precision agriculture, grid optimization, industrial robotics. Australia's largest industries — mining, agriculture, energy — are prime deployment environments, giving Australian startups a testing ground US companies cannot easily access.
03
Supply Chain Sovereignty
Building Western-allied supply chains for critical minerals and energy infrastructure is now bipartisan US policy. The Critical Minerals Agreement, AUKUS, and DOD procurement all point to a multi-decade investment cycle in exactly the sectors where Bandera operates.
The Opportunity

The Seed-Stage Funding Gap

At Series A and beyond, international capital actively competes for the best Australian hardtech deals. Growth-stage funds — Main Sequence, Virescent, Climate Tech Partners, Adamantem — are well-capitalized and active. Infrastructure investors like Wollemi and HMC fund first-of-kind projects.

But at seed, the options are extremely limited. The gap is filled by angel syndicates and government grants — neither of which can provide the speed, conviction, or follow-on support that contrarian early-stage investing requires.

This creates Bandera's structural advantage. With minimal competition at seed, the fund has its pick of deals, commands favorable terms, and builds relationships with founders before other institutional capital arrives.

The strategy: Invest early in Australia. Position portfolio companies for later-stage US and international capital at significantly higher valuations.

Investment Philosophy
"Impact is not a strategy."
Large enterprises adopt new technologies to improve the bottom line. Solutions must deliver commercial ROI independent of environmental benefit. The most successful companies are "double-green": they deliver both financial returns and positive environmental outcomes, making them attractive regardless of regulatory shifts.
"Back the jockey, not just the horse."
At the seed stage, the technology is early and the market is uncertain. The founder's ability to adapt, sell, and execute is the primary driver of outcomes.
"Selling is believing."
Hardtech founders tend to over-index on R&D and under-index on commercialization. Bandera actively pushes portfolio companies toward early customer engagement and revenue.

Risk Factors

Key Risks

Solo GP / Key Person Risk
The fund depends on Jeremy Atkin as sole decision-maker. Mitigated by a strong Advisory Board with fiduciary responsibility for managing the portfolio to liquidity in the event of GP incapacitation, and an operationally lean fund structure designed to minimize complexity.
Early-Stage Hardtech Risk
Pre-revenue hardtech companies carry meaningful technology and commercialization risk. Mitigated by diversification across 20–25 companies, deep sector expertise, and a philosophy that prioritizes founders with commercial orientation.
Liquidity Timeline
Hardtech typically has longer paths to exit than software. Mitigated by 10-year fund life with extensions, and active pursuit of secondary sale opportunities in parallel with funding rounds.
Currency Risk
The fund is AUD-denominated. US investors bear AUD/USD exposure. The Australian dollar's historical correlation with commodity prices may provide a natural hedge given sector focus. Currency mix shifts as portfolio companies expand into the US.
Regulatory / Policy Risk
Bandera invests only in companies whose business models are commercially viable independent of subsidies or regulatory incentives. Environmental benefits are a tailwind, not the foundation.
